I'm an astronomy graduate student interested in time domain science. I approach this research in a unique way: with a focus at writing flexible and reusable codes to fully exploit large datasets of transient events to further our understanding as a community.

I'm currently a second year student and NSF graduate research fellow at the University of Arizona, Steward Observatory. I primarily focus on multiwavelength followup of tidal disruption events, when a star is torn apart by a supermassive black hole, with Dr. Kate Alexander as part of the Steward Observatory Novel Astrophysical Transient Alliance (SONATA). Specifically, I am focused on improving our understanding of galactic nuclei, black holes, and accretion processes by studying large numbers of tidal disruption events. This has only recently become possible with the increase in numbers of known tidal disruption events.

In my spare time, I also work with the Searches After Gravitational waves Using ARizona Observatories (SAGUARO) collaboration to perform multiwavelength followup of gravitational waves, searching for an electromagnetic counterpart. I've been focused on helping build up their infrastructure in preparation for the next promising gravitational wave detection.

In spring 2023, I graduated Summa Cum Laude from Siena College with a B.S. Degree. In undergraduate, I double majored in Physics and Applied Data Science and obtained minors in Astronomy and Mathematics. During these four years, I also discovered my strong interests in astronomy, big data, data science, and programming. Throughout undergraduate, I explored a wide variety of research interests including working with the Dark Energy Spectroscopic Instrument, Berkeley Search for Extraterrestrial Intelligence, and The University of Hawaii Institute for Astronomy.
